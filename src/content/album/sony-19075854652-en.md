## A Gift to Himself, Sixty Years in the Making

At age four, Yo-Yo Ma learned eight notes.

<!-- fact: Ma began studying cello at age four under his father, Dr. Hiao-Tsiun Ma. The first piece he learned was the opening bars of Bach's Cello Suite No. 1 in G major, BWV 1007 -- the Prélude -->

Not a symphony. Not a concerto. Eight notes from the Prélude of Bach's first cello suite. From those eight notes, he walked a journey of sixty years.

<!-- fiction: narrative metaphor using "walked a journey" to echo Ma's lifelong relationship with this music -->

## The Third and Final Recording

In 2018, at sixty-two, Yo-Yo Ma walked into a recording studio to record Bach's complete cello suites for the third and final time.

<!-- fact: Sony Classical released Six Evolutions — Bach: Cello Suites on August 17, 2018. Ma has publicly stated this is his final complete recording of the suites -->

The first was in 1983, at twenty-seven — young and ambitious. The second in 1997, at forty-two — a period of transition. The third, at sixty-two — he calls it his last.

<!-- fact: Previous complete recordings: 1983 (CBS Masterworks / Sony), 1997 (Sony Classical). The 2018 recording was marketed as his final complete set -->

I don't usually believe musicians when they say "last time." It's often just a setup for the next comeback. But listen to the Sarabande of Suite No. 5 on this recording, and you'll understand why he said it.

<!-- fiction: personal listening observation, literary rather than reportorial -->

## The G Major Prélude, Like I Remembered It

I've heard countless versions of the Prélude from Suite No. 1. Casals' raw intensity. Starker's architectural precision. Rostropovich's deep emotional sweep.

<!-- fact: Pablo Casals (1876–1973) discovered and popularized the Bach cello suites, recording them in the 1930s; Janos Starker (1924–2013) was renowned for precision; Mstislav Rostropovich (1927–2007) for emotional depth -->

But Ma's version is different. His cello doesn't sound like it's being played — it sounds like it's remembering itself. The tempo is unhurried, with enough space between each note for you to breathe. It doesn't feel like a performance. It feels like a man talking to himself.

<!-- fiction: subjective listening experience, literary music description -->

## The Sarabande, a Dance for One

If you don't have time for all six suites — though you absolutely should — at least listen to the Sarabande from Suite No. 5 in C minor.

<!-- fact: Suite No. 5, BWV 1011, Sarabande is the fourth movement and widely considered the emotional heart of the suite -->

It is the slowest of slow movements. Just the cello, alone, without harmony, without accompaniment, barely any double stops. A single melodic line, walking a tightrope through the troubled key of C minor.

<!-- fact: C minor in Bach's time carried connotations of pathos and gravity. The Sarabande originated as a Spanish dance but here is transformed into an extremely slow movement -->

Every time I hear this Sarabande, I think of Yo-Yo Ma's 2020 concert in Taipei.

<!-- fact: November 10, 2020, Yo-Yo Ma performed at the Taipei Music Center as part of The Bach Project — his first live concert since the pandemic began -->

Most concert halls around the world were still dark that November. Ma flew from New York to Taipei, quarantined for seven days, walked onto the stage of Taipei Music Center. Four thousand masked people sat in the dark — some crying, some laughing, some just letting the tears soak their masks in silence.

<!-- fact: Ma received special approval from Taiwan health authorities for a shortened 7-day quarantine; the concert had approximately 4,000 attendees, as reported by Central News Agency and Taipei Times -->

<!-- fiction: specific audience reactions are literary imagination to heighten emotional atmosphere -->

He played Suites Nos. 1, 5, and 6 in full. He also played Zhao Jiping's "Summer in the High Grassland" and George Crumb's cello sonata. But what I remember most is the Sarabande from Suite No. 5 — because nobody coughed, nobody rustled their program, nobody moved. A thousand people held their breath together in the dark.

<!-- fact: November 10, 2020 program included Bach Suites No. 1, 5, 6, plus Zhao Jiping's "Summer in the High Grassland" and George Crumb's cello sonata -->

<!-- fiction: the silence of the audience is a literary touch, though acoustically plausible -->

## Bach Is Not Meant to Be Understood

Ma once said something I've never forgotten: "You can never understand Bach the same way twice, because you are never the same person."

<!-- fact: Yo-Yo Ma said this in a press interview ahead of his September 2025 Taipei concert, quoted by several media outlets including Mirror Media and Liberty Times -->

That is what makes this recording so moving. It doesn't just capture how a sixty-two-year-old man plays Bach. It captures how each listener, at different stages of their life, hears Bach.

<!-- fiction: author's reflection, critical commentary -->

I didn't understand this at four. I doubt Ma did either, at four. But sixty years teaches you a few things. Or rather, Bach teaches you a few things.

<!-- fiction: personal reflection echoing Ma's story, using literary device for resonance -->

This CD is spinning in my player right now. The Gigue from Suite No. 6 is about to begin — D major, bright, joyful, like sunlight sneaking through the gap in the curtains. I press repeat and listen again.

## Comparative Versions

For those wishing to explore different interpretations of Bach's cello suites: Casals (EMI / Naxos, 1936–1939) offers the historic, pioneering recording; Starker (RCA / Mercury, 1990–1992) delivers structural clarity; Rostropovich (EMI, 1995) provides emotional intensity. Ma's own 1983 recording, reissued as "The 1983 Sessions," reveals a very different, more energetic approach from his youth.

<!-- fact: Casals 1936–1939 EMI recording is historically the first complete set; Starker 1990–1992 Mercury is prized for precision; Rostropovich 1995 EMI is emotionally driven -->
