## Album Overview

Before he became the global phenomenon who filled stadiums with the Three Tenors, before the world knew his name alongside Domingo and Carreras — there was a younger Pavarotti. A voice so extraordinary that a single aria at the Metropolitan Opera in 1972 earned him a title that would follow him for life.

This compilation brings together seven recordings Pavarotti made between 1967 and 1972, capturing the purest, most radiant phase of his career. Every track is a showcase for what made him special: a voice of extraordinary beauty, a natural musicality that seemed effortless, and a high C that could stop time.

## The Tracks

### Ah! Mes Amis — Pour Mon Ame (Donizetti: La Fille du Régiment)

This is the aria that changed everything. Nine high Cs in sixty seconds, each one a test that separates the merely good from the immortal. Pavarotti's 1972 Met performance of this aria became the stuff of legend — the audience erupted after the first eight and went into a frenzy at the ninth. In this studio recording, you can hear why. The clarity, the ping, the way each C seems to float in space before the next one arrives.

### Spirto Gentil (Donizetti: La Favorite)

A bel canto aria of sublime tenderness. Pavarotti's voice floats effortlessly over Donizetti's long-breathed phrases, the gentle spirit of the title captured in every note. The line is pure, the vibrato natural — this is bel canto singing at its finest.

### Di Quella Pira (Verdi: Il Trovatore)

Manrico's defiant call to arms — one of the most famous tenor arias in all of Verdi. Pavarotti unleashes the full power of his voice here, the heroic sweep of the melody matched by the brilliant top notes. The cabaletta is thrilling, each phrase building toward that climactic high C that brings down the house.

### Di Rigori Armato (R. Strauss: Der Rosenkavalier)

A surprising inclusion — Strauss's Italian tenor aria from a German opera. Pavarotti brings a lightness and elegance to this recitative and aria that reveals his versatility. The legato is exquisite, the pianissimo high notes a display of control that few tenors could match.

### O Muto Asil (Rossini: William Tell)

Arnold's great scene from Rossini's epic — a long, demanding aria that requires both lyrical grace and heroic power. Pavarotti's breath control is breathtaking: he spins the long melody of the slow section with seemingly endless reserves, then launches into the cabaletta with thrilling abandon.

### A Te, O Cara (Bellini: I Puritani)

Arturo's entrance aria from Bellini's last opera. This is pure bel canto — long, arching phrases, delicate ornamentation, and a high D-flat that Pavarotti delivers with gossamer delicacy. It is perhaps the most beautiful track on the album, showing the lyrical, tender side of his art.

### Che Gelida Manina (Puccini: La Bohème)

Rodolfo's Act I aria — one of the most beloved tenor arias in opera. Pavarotti's version is a benchmark: the conversational opening, the growing warmth as he describes Mimì's hand, the soaring climax on 'speranza' that somehow combines tenderness with immense power. This is Pavarotti at his most human, his most lovable.

## Listening Notes

This compilation is essential listening for anyone who wants to hear Pavarotti before he became a brand — when he was simply a young tenor with the most beautiful voice in the world. The recordings were made in London between 1967 and 1972, with various orchestras and conductors. The sound quality varies slightly between tracks, but the voice is consistently ravishing.
