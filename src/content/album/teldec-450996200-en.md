## That Night, We Were All There

<!-- fiction: first-person narrative device -->
In the summer of 1994, our old TV had a blown speaker.

But when Pavarotti sang the first note of "Nessun dorma," the distortion didn't matter. I don't remember exactly how old I was, but I remember the image: three men in tuxedos standing in the middle of a baseball stadium, the Los Angeles twilight behind them, 56,000 people in the seats before them. And a number I couldn't comprehend at the time: 1.3 billion. All watching the same thing at the same time.
<!-- fact: 1994/7/16, Dodger Stadium, three tenors in tuxedos -->
<!-- fact: 1.3 billion per NYT 1996 article (cited by Wikipedia) -->
<!-- approx: 56,000 = Dodger Stadium capacity -->

It was the night before the World Cup final. It was an age before the internet ruled everything. It was the moment the Three Tenors proved that Rome 1990 was not a fluke.
<!-- fact: World Cup final was July 17, 1994 -->

## From Rome to Los Angeles

Four years earlier, at the Baths of Caracalla, they had made history. The album sold ten million copies — the best-selling classical recording of all time.
<!-- fact: Decca 430 433-2 sold 10M+ copies -->

The hardest thing about a miracle is doing it twice.

They chose Dodger Stadium.

## Three Voices, One Night

Carreras went first. He sang Massenet's "O souverain, ô juge, ô père" from Le Cid — his voice as clear as a man who had stared down leukemia and won. Every note was a declaration: I am still here.
<!-- fact: Carreras sang Le Cid aria -->
<!-- fact: diagnosed with leukemia 1987, recovered -->
<!-- fiction: voice description, "I am still here" -->

Domingo followed with "Granada." His voice is warm, like Spanish afternoon sun. He sang each high note not as a conquest, but as a gift.
<!-- fact: Domingo sang Granada (Agustín Lara) -->
<!-- fiction: similes and metaphors -->

Then Pavarotti. He sang "Non ti scordar di me" — "Don't forget me." How fitting. His voice rose from somewhere deep inside him, through the Dodger Stadium night, through the television signal, through our blown speaker, and into me.

## Around the World

The second half was a seventeen-minute medley arranged by Lalo Schifrin — a journey from Hollywood to Brazil, from French chanson to Italian folk songs.
<!-- fact: Lalo Schifrin arrangement, 17-minute medley -->

They sang "My Way." They sang "Moon River." They sang "Singin' in the Rain." Three opera tenors singing Broadway and pop standards. On paper, it sounds absurd. But in that stadium, with 56,000 people cheering and 1.3 billion watching, it made perfect sense.
<!-- fact: all three songs part of the Hollywood medley -->
<!-- fiction: "sounds absurd", "made perfect sense" = subjective voice -->

Because music isn't about categories. It's about connection.
<!-- fiction: author's reflection -->

## The Encore

They came back to sing "Nessun dorma" together.
<!-- fact: encore was Nessun dorma trio -->

Pavarotti began. Domingo joined. Carreras followed. Three voices, weaving together, rising to that final high B. Dodger Stadium's roof didn't exist anymore — there was only a dome of sound, covering the entire planet.
