## Three Voices

On October 3, 2010, the O2 Arena in London held sixteen thousand people.

<!-- fact: Two performances on October 3, 2010 at the O2 Arena (13:30 and 19:00). The O2 has a maximum capacity of 20,000; concert seating was approximately 16,000 -->

The lights went down. A man walked onto the stage from the side. He wore prison rags, walking slowly, each step as if treading on broken glass.

Then he opened his mouth—

"Look down, look down, don't look 'em in the eye…"

Alfie Boe's voice isn't the kind of beautiful that makes you forget the story. It's the opposite—rough, tired, like a man who really spent nineteen years in a chain gang.

<!-- fiction: subjective description of Boe's vocal quality -->

He's one of England's finest musical theatre tenors. But the first time I heard him sing Jean Valjean, I didn't hear singing. I heard a man asking for rescue.

<!-- fact: Alfie Boe (1973–) played Jean Valjean in the 25th anniversary concert. He also performed Rodolfo in La Bohème on Broadway (Tony Award for Best Revival) -->

But the voice that floored me that night belonged to someone else.

Norm Lewis's Javert — the song "Stars" — from the first note to the last, his low register didn't waver once. That certainty wasn't an act. He truly believed. The law is the law. People can be sorted into good and bad.

<!-- fact: "Stars" is Javert's solo expressing his absolute faith in law and justice. Norm Lewis (1963–) is an American Broadway veteran -->

"There, out in the darkness, a fugitive running…"

His voice cut through the arena like a laser beam. Sixteen thousand people stopped breathing.

<!-- fiction: metaphorical dramatization of the atmosphere -->

The third voice was Lea Salonga's "I Dreamed a Dream."

If there's a song on this planet that needs no introduction, it's this one. But when Lea Salonga sang it, I realized I'd never really understood it before.

<!-- fact: "I Dreamed a Dream" is Fantine's lament about lost dreams and broken promises. Lea Salonga (1971–) played Fantine in the 25th anniversary concert -->

Her Fantine wasn't just crying about her fate. She was angry — angry at a world that had chewed her up and left no dignity behind. That line "He took my childhood in his stride, but he was gone when autumn came" — the way she sang "gone" held not just grief, but the clarity of someone who knows she's been betrayed.

<!-- fiction: interpretative analysis of vocal performance, literary in nature -->

## The Children of the Barricade

When "Do You Hear the People Sing?" erupted, five hundred people filled the stage. The O2's lights blazed, and the audience roared back: "Will you join in our crusade?"

<!-- fact: The concert featured over 500 actors and musicians. "Do You Hear the People Sing?" is the revolutionary anthem that opens Act II -->

But Ramin Karimloo's Enjolras — that was something else entirely.

He stood atop the barricade, red flag in hand, singing "Red and Black." His voice wasn't asking anyone to follow. It was commanding.

<!-- fact: Ramin Karimloo (1978–) played Enjolras. "Red and Black" is the student revolutionaries' ensemble piece about action vs. inaction -->

Fifteen years later, I learned, he would stand on a stage in Taipei, channeling the same force into another song.

<!-- fact: Ramin Karimloo made his Taiwan debut in September 2025, starring in The Reunion musical concert at Taipei Music Center -->

## Nine Minutes of Curtain Calls

The curtain call after the show lasted nine minutes.

Not because the actors kept bowing. But because when the original 1985 cast members walked on stage, the audience wouldn't let them leave.

<!-- fact: Original 1985 London cast members joined the finale: Colm Wilkinson (original Valjean), Michael Ball (original Marius), Frances Ruffelle (original Éponine), Alun Armstrong (original Thénardier), and others -->

Colm Wilkinson — the first Valjean, then sixty-six years old — sang the final line of "Bring Him Home." His high notes weren't perfect anymore. But they still held.

<!-- fact: Colm Wilkinson (1944–) originated Jean Valjean in the 1985 West End premiere. Frequently cited as the definitive Valjean -->

Michael Ball — twenty-five years earlier a twenty-three-year-old Marius — now had grey hair, but his smile was still that young man's smile.

"One day more!" the entire hall sang.

<!-- fact: Michael Ball (1962–) originated Marius in 1985. He became one of the UK's most beloved musical theatre stars. "One Day More" is the Act I finale ensemble number -->

I pressed pause and got a glass of water. My hands were shaking. I'm not sure if it was from emotion, or from shouting along too loudly.

<!-- fiction: personal anecdote, literary device -->

## Why This Blu-ray

If you own a Blu-ray player, this might be the best musical Blu-ray bargain you'll ever find.

<!-- opinion: subjective recommendation -->

The video isn't perfect — bright stage lighting softens the wide shots. But the audio is. The DTS-HD Master Audio 5.1 track captures every breath, every footstep, every moment of silence.

<!-- fact: Blu-ray features VC-1 encoded 1080p video and DTS-HD Master Audio 5.1. blu-ray.com review notes wide shots suffer from lighting bloom but close-ups are excellent -->

More importantly, it captures a night that will never happen again. Two shows. Five hundred performers. Twenty-five years of tears, all pressed onto a silver disc.

You don't need to know the musical. You don't need to be able to sing along. You just need to sit down and listen.
