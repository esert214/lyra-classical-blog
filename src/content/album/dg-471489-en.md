## Fate, at the Door

Beethoven said those four notes — three shorts and a long — were fate knocking at the door.
<!-- fact: attributed to Beethoven via his secretary Schindler -->

I never believed that.

<!-- fact: DG 471 489-2, recorded 2001 at Berlin Philharmonie -->
<!-- fact: Abbado (1933–2014) was chief conductor of Berlin Philharmonic at the time -->
But when Abbado returned to this symphony near the end of his career, knowing his body was failing him, and left us this recording — I finally understood. Those notes weren't knocking on a door. They were knocking on his chest.

## Abbado's C Major

In 2000, Abbado was diagnosed with stomach cancer. He had surgery, part of his stomach removed. The following year, he walked into the Berlin Philharmonie and recorded the "Fate" symphony.
<!-- fact: Abbado diagnosed with gastric cancer 2000, underwent surgery -->
<!-- fact: this recording is from 2001, part of his complete DG cycle -->

If fate has a sound, what does it look like?

Beethoven was going deaf when he wrote this symphony. He couldn't hear the world, yet he wrote the most famous melody in music.
<!-- fact: Beethoven composed Symphony No. 5 between 1804–1808, his hearing severely deteriorated -->
Abbado lived another thirteen years after this recording. He founded the Lucerne Festival Orchestra. He left us even more music.

Maybe fate isn't here to destroy. Maybe it's just asking: are you ready?

## Movement I

Three shorts and a long. It's not sadness. It's not anger. It's an awakening.
<!-- fact: Allegro con brio, C minor -->
<!-- fiction: subjective listening impressions follow -->
The Berlin Philharmonic's opening is as precise as a heartbeat. Abbado's tempo is neither fast nor slow — no Karajan-like imperial authority, no Bernstein theatrical drama. Just a man saying: I have arrived.

Listen to that oboe solo in the recapitulation. A man standing up in the ruins.

Abbado gave that moment room to breathe.

## Movement II

Andante con moto. Double theme and variations.
<!-- fact: Andante con moto, A-flat major -->
The lyrical theme emerges from violas and cellos, like a conversation at night. Abbado draws a warmth from the strings that feels like body temperature, not Berlin coldness.

Then the clarinet joins. That slightly husky timbre — the signature of Berlin Philharmonic woodwinds.
<!-- fact: Berlin Philharmonic woodwinds are renowned for their warm, rich tone -->

## Movement III

Scherzo. But not humorous.
<!-- fact: Scherzo: Allegro, C minor -->
The mysterious pulse from low strings, like vibrations from underground. The brass's short cries, searching in the dark.

The trio section — cellos and double basses in dialogue — shows a rare pastoral side of Beethoven. It sounds like an old farmer sitting on a ridge, smoking, looking at the distance, saying nothing.

## Movement IV

C major. The key of victory.
<!-- fact: Allegro, C major — first symphony to feature trombones -->
Trombones appear for the first time in symphonic history — a landmark moment. Brass brilliance and string momentum, like sunlight breaking through storm clouds.

Abbado doesn't let the orchestra rush. He treats victory not as ecstasy, but as certainty.

Sometimes certainty is harder than ecstasy.

## After

Abbado died in Bologna in 2014, aged 81.
<!-- fact: Abbado died January 20, 2014 in Bologna -->

This recording was later collected into the "Abbado Complete" edition on DG.

But I prefer to simply put it on, late at night, and listen from start to finish.

Those four notes — three shorts and a long — travel across two hundred years, through a deaf Beethoven, through a cancer-stricken Abbado, to reach me.

If fate is really knocking.

This time, I open the door.
