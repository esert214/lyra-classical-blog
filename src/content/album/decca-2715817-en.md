## Album Overview

On September 18 and 19, 2008, inside Boston Symphony Hall, a hundred people held their breath. Perhaps they were waiting for Chris Botti's trumpet to sound the first note. Perhaps they were waiting to see which of the evening's legendary guests would walk on stage first.

Then Sting appeared. Then Yo-Yo Ma. Then Josh Groban, John Mayer, and Steven Tyler of Aerosmith.

The evening began with Schubert's Ave Maria and ended with Time to Say Goodbye — traversing jazz, classical, rock, pop, and film scores along the way. It sounds like an impossible dream lineup, but it actually happened in Boston in September 2008.

This live recording went platinum in the United States and earned three Grammy nominations: Best Pop Instrumental Album, Best Instrumental Arrangement, and Best Long Form Music Video.

## Track Highlights

### Ave Maria

Schubert's most beloved melody, introduced by the warm strings of the Boston Pops Orchestra. Botti's trumpet is unhurried here — pure, simple lines that build a sacred, transcendent atmosphere.

### When I Fall In Love

A romantic jazz standard. Botti begins with a tender melody, then shifts into a fiery improvisation section where each member of his sextet takes turns soloing — revealing the chemistry and energy of his touring band.

### Seven Days (feat. Sting & Dominic Miller)

Sting arrives with his classic Seven Days, immediately lifting the room. Sting's voice and Botti's trumpet intertwine, with Dominic Miller's guitar weaving through the spaces — a perfect meeting of pop songcraft and jazz spontaneity.

### Emmanuel (feat. Lucia Micarelli)

Violinist Lucia Micarelli's solo is perhaps the most heartbreaking moment of the entire concert. Michel Colombier's piece builds from whispered beginnings to a sweeping climax, earning a Grammy nomination for Best Instrumental Arrangement.

### I've Got You Under My Skin (feat. Katharine McPhee)

Katharine McPhee reimagines Cole Porter's standard with a contemporary pop sensibility, and Botti's improvisational responses are playful and joyful — a delightful musical conversation.

### Cinema Paradiso (feat. Yo-Yo Ma)

The dream pairing of the night: Chris Botti's trumpet and Yo-Yo Ma's cello in dialogue over Ennio Morricone's timeless melody. The warmth of the trumpet and the depth of the cello achieve perfect equilibrium in this piece about memory and nostalgia.

### Broken Vow (feat. Josh Groban)

Josh Groban's rich voice brings new life to this Lara Fabian classic. Botti's weeping trumpet fills in the spaces between vocal phrases, creating a deeply moving dialogue.

### Flamenco Sketches

A tribute to Miles Davis, whose modal jazz classic is reimagined with orchestral arrangements. Botti preserves the original's loose structure and atmospheric quality while adding his own warm tonal signature.

### Glad To Be Unhappy (feat. John Mayer)

John Mayer makes a surprising appearance as a pure jazz vocalist on this Rodgers & Hart standard. Known for pop and rock, he reveals an unexpected gift for jazz interpretation.

### Hallelujah

Leonard Cohen's immortal song, rendered as a spare, heartfelt trumpet solo. No flashy technique, only pure emotion — which is precisely what makes Botti's playing so compelling.

### Smile (feat. Steven Tyler)

Steven Tyler's entrance changes the room's energy. He first roars through Aerosmith's Cryin', then surprises everyone with Charlie Chaplin's Smile — revealing the tender side of a hard-rock legend.

### If I Ever Lose My Faith In You (feat. Sting & Dominic Miller)

Sting returns for this second appearance, performing his classic with Botti. It sets the stage for the evening's finale — Sting performing two songs back to back, a testament to his deep friendship with the trumpeter.

### Time to Say Goodbye

The closing piece: a solo trumpet and orchestra version. No operatic tenor, no chorus — just Botti's unadorned horn. In this farewell song, his trumpet speaks with more penetrating power than any human voice could muster.

## Listening Notes

If you discovered Chris Botti through his live performances in Taipei's National Concert Hall — this album will take you back to that night. If you haven't heard him yet — start here, because it encompasses the full spectrum of his musical identity: jazz, classical, pop, and film music, each facet on display. This is a live album with no dull moments — a beautiful testament to how genre boundaries dissolve when music is made this well.
