## The Night the Hall Trembled

On October 2, 2011, London's Royal Albert Hall was packed to the rafters — over five thousand seats filled. Cinemas around the world were carrying the live broadcast: London, New York, Tokyo, Taipei. Hundreds of thousands of people, in the same evening, watching the same chandelier rise.

<!-- fact: Three performances took place on October 1–2, 2011, with the third (Oct 2) broadcast live to cinemas worldwide. The Royal Albert Hall seats 5,272 -->

<!-- fiction: "Hundreds of thousands" is an evocative approximation of global audience numbers -->

It was the twenty-fifth birthday of The Phantom of the Opera. On October 9, 1986, the musical had premiered at Her Majesty's Theatre in London. No one has been able to take that chandelier out of our hearts since.

<!-- fact: Phantom premiered at Her Majesty's Theatre, London, on October 9, 1986 -->

## The Phantom Speaks

When Ramin Karimloo walked onto the stage as the Phantom, there wasn't a sound in the hall.

<!-- fact: Ramin Karimloo (1978–), Iranian-Canadian actor, performed the Phantom in London's West End from 2007–2009 and originated the role in Love Never Dies (West End premiere, 2010) -->

Before he sang a single note, he just stood there — the white half-mask hiding his face. That silence was enough to remind everyone: standing on this stage was one of the youngest Phantoms in the show's twenty-five-year history.

<!-- fiction: narrative dramatization of the silence for emotional effect -->

Then he opened his mouth.

"In sleep he sang to me... in dreams he came..."

<!-- fact: "In sleep he sang to me" opens the musical, sung by Christine in her dressing room. The Phantom's voice is heard but he is not yet visible on stage -->

## Christine's High E-Flat

When Sierra Boggess sang the first line of "Think of Me," that ascending E-flat — it sounded cleaner than anything that had a right to come out of a human throat.

<!-- fact: "Think of Me" is Christine's first full solo. The high E-flat is a pivotal note in the aria -->

<!-- fiction: subjective description of vocal quality -->

I've watched this Blu-ray many times since. Every time, I rewind that note.

I don't know how to describe it. It doesn't sound like singing. It sounds like something very bright falling from a great height and landing exactly where it was supposed to.

<!-- fiction: metaphorical description, literary in nature -->

This Christine is among the finest the production has seen in twenty-five years. That's not just my opinion — it's what the critics said, and it's what everyone who buys this Blu-ray discovers.

<!-- fact: Sierra Boggess received widespread critical acclaim for her performance, though "among the finest" is a general summation -->

## The Finale — Sarah Arrives

A hundred and fifty minutes later, the musical ended. The audience rose to its feet.

Then Andrew Lloyd Webber walked on stage. He said a few words — about gratitude, about how fast time flies — and then he called out a name. The hall erupted.

<!-- fact: Andrew Lloyd Webber attended the performance and gave a speech after the curtain call -->

Sarah Brightman — the original Christine from twenty-five years earlier, Webber's then-wife — walked out in a red dress.

<!-- fact: Sarah Brightman originated the role of Christine in the 1986 West End premiere. She and Lloyd Webber divorced in 1990 -->

She sang "The Phantom of the Opera." And four former Phantoms — Colm Wilkinson, Anthony Warlow, John Owen-Jones, Peter Jöback — took turns singing with her: "Sing once again with me our strange duet..."

<!-- fact: During the finale, Sarah Brightman sang the title song with four former Phantoms. The performance also featured an appearance by original cast members -->

Michael Crawford was on stage too. The first Phantom, 1986. He didn't sing. He just stood there and smiled.

<!-- fact: Michael Crawford (1942–), the original 1986 Phantom, appeared but did not sing during the finale -->

Those thirty seconds might have been the quietest of the entire night. No one singing. No one clapping. Just five men in black suits and one woman in red, standing there, letting time flow backwards.

<!-- fiction: emotional dramatization of the finale, literary in nature -->

## Comparative Versions

The 2004 film adaptation (directed by Joel Schumacher, starring Gerard Butler and Emmy Rossum) offers a different visual interpretation of Phantom. The 1986 original cast recording (Michael Crawford, Sarah Brightman) preserves the definitive original sound. The Love Never Dies original cast recording provides a natural extension for those wanting more of the story.
